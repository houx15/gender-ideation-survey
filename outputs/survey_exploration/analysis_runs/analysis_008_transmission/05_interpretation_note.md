# analysis_008 — Interpretation note

## What the results say (RQ 5.7)
From `04_result_table.csv` / `whose_ideation_comparison.csv` (5,564 / 4,120 children).

1. **Both parents transmit gender ideology, strongly and roughly equally.** With both
   in the same model: mother +0.126 / father +0.153 (2014), mother +0.102 / father +0.116
   (2020); all t ≥ 5.8. A 1-unit (full-scale) more-traditional parent is associated with
   a ~0.10–0.15 more-traditional child.
2. **The father's coefficient is, if anything, slightly larger than the mother's** — the
   opposite of a "mother matters more for transmission" expectation. The two are close,
   though; a formal equality test is still needed.
3. **Same-/cross-gender paths are suggestive but not significant.** Raw correlations hint
   at same-gender transmission (2014: mother→daughter 0.154 > mother→son 0.120;
   father→son 0.163), but the `mother×daughter` / `father×daughter` interactions are not
   statistically significant.
4. **Daughters are markedly less traditional than sons**, net of parents
   (child_female −0.06 in 2014, **−0.12 in 2020**) — and this gap grew between waves,
   echoing the cohort crossover (analysis_002).

## How this bears on "does women's ideology matter more in the family?"
A clear pattern is emerging across analyses:
- **Where a woman enacts her own behavior** (her housework, her labour-force participation),
  **her ideology dominates** (analysis_004/005/007).
- **Where the outcome is the child's beliefs**, **mother and father transmit about equally**
  (father slightly more here) — women's ideology is *not* uniquely powerful for transmission.

So "women's ideology matters more" holds for *self-enacted* family practice, but not for
the *ideological socialization* of children, where both parents count.

## Threats / caveats
- Co-residence selection likely inflates transmission; cross-sectional; no weights.
- mother vs father difference not formally tested; CFPS index reliability modest.
