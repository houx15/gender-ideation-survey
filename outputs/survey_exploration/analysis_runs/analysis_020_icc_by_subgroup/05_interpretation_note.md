# analysis_020 — Interpretation note

## Sibling ICC by subgroup (`01_descriptive_table.csv`)

| Subgroup | 2014 ICC | 2020 ICC | parent ideology explains (2014 / 2020) |
|----------|---------:|---------:|----------------------------------------|
| all multi-child | 0.260 | 0.196 | 11.4% / 6.4% |
| rural families | 0.262 | **0.217** | 15.7% / 2.6% |
| urban families | 0.255 | **0.168** | 7.5% / 11.0% |
| older sibship | 0.187 | 0.147 | 14.9% / 6.1% |
| younger sibship | **0.298** | **0.170** | 9.2% / 6.0% |

## What this shows
1. **Ideology is family-clustered in every context** (ICC ≈ 0.15–0.30): a sizeable share of
   the variance is between-family everywhere.
2. **Modestly stronger clustering in rural families** (2020: 0.217 vs 0.168 urban) and in
   **younger sibships** (2014: 0.298 vs 0.187 older). Younger siblings resemble each other
   more — consistent with shared recent environment before divergence; rural families show
   more homogeneous within-family ideology (less heterogeneous exposure).
3. **Parents' measured ideology explains only a small, variable slice (3–16%)** of sibling
   resemblance in *every* subgroup — never the majority. Notably in rural 2020 it explains
   almost nothing (2.6%) despite the highest ICC, pointing to **community/shared environment**
   (local norms, schooling, economy) rather than the specific parental attitude as the main
   driver of family-level ideology clustering there.

## Bottom line
The "regeneration" of ideology runs largely through the **broad shared family/community
environment**, with the parents' explicit gender attitudes a real but minor channel — and
this is true across rural/urban and cohort subgroups. Family clustering is somewhat stronger
in rural and younger-sibling settings.

## Caveats
ICC = variance share, not causal; cohort proxied by family mean child age (coarse,
period-confounded); selected multi-child sample; no weights. See method note.

## Next steps

the provincial ideology climate as a between-family predictor (which may explain the rural
